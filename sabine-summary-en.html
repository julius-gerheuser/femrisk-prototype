Your health factors

Based on your questionnaire, FemRisk identified the following factors in your personal history that are relevant to cardiovascular health in women.

Factor 1 — Preeclampsia (at age 29)

In international guidelines, preeclampsia during pregnancy is considered a factor linked to elevated cardiovascular risk later in life. Women with a history of preeclampsia may benefit from earlier and more regular cardiovascular check-ups.

Factor 2 — Early menopause (at age 47)

Menopause before age 50, and especially before age 45, is linked to changes in cardiovascular health. The earlier loss of the protective estrogen effect can accelerate the development of common cardiovascular risk factors.

Factor 3 — Family history of cardiovascular disease

You indicated that a close family member had cardiovascular disease before age 65. Family history is a well-established factor in cardiovascular health.

What this means for you

Your personal history includes several factors that, taken together, are relevant to your long-term cardiovascular health. These factors are well documented in medical guidelines — but are not captured by standard check-ups.

This does not mean you are ill or will become ill. It means you have good reason to actively look after your cardiovascular health and to have an informed conversation with your doctor.
